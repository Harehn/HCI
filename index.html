Goals

Our solution aims to enhance the accessibility of educational websites for the users with disabilities such as visual impairment, and learning impediments. According to the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1, an accessible website should be perceivable, operable, understandable and robust. These would be important characteristics that account for the success of our solution. However, the robustness, which is the compatibility with various “user agents” such as browsers and assistive technologies, of a website would be hard to test and measure at the early stages with low-fidelity prototype only. Therefore, for the current phase of testing, it is proposed that our solution should come up with the following characteristics and hence usability goals:

Easy to perceive

Goal: “Users should be able to perceive correct information based on different visual or non-visual elements”

Allowing users to get correct information would be an important basis of an educational website. However, people with visual impairment such as colour blindness may have problems reading the color coding on the website, which may cause misinterpretation of information. Therefore, it is crucial to make use of different visual and non-visual elements, such as the use of color and theme, to enhance the accessibility and ensure that the web content is easy for the users with disabilities and learning impediments to perceive.

Easy to use

Goal: “Users should be able to navigate the website to perform all possible tasks without any difficulties after a reasonable time of exploration”

Having complicated navigation or too many accessible pages may cause confusion that hinders users’ learning progress. Consistency, clear titles and labels are important for users to navigate the website, access the information they want and perform different learning activities.

Understandable

Goal: “Users should be able to read and comprehend the web content with reasonable efforts”

There would be many different learning materials, such as articles, research papers, guidelines, posted on an educational website that requires the user to read and hence make the learning progress. However, people with learning disabilities and cognitive disabilities, such as ADHD and dyslexia, could have problems reading long and complex sentences, or focusing on reading long paragraphs. Therefore, ensuring the web content to be understandable for them would be one of the goals.

Flexible to customize

Goal: “Users should be able to customize different elements based on their personal preference that helps better use of the website to learn efficiently and effectively”

People have different preferences on information layout. If users could perform customization, such as visual clutter adjustment, theme colour customization and text size modification, that fits their individual needs, it could help them get access to the web content and be engaged to the system, especially for the people with visual impairment.

Our goal is to explore the difficulties they experience, including navigating through content, understanding text, and interpreting visual elements. By understanding these challenges, we can develop tailored solutions to improve accessibility.

Benchmark Tasks:

We have created benchmark tasks to evaluate different aspects of our project, such as usability, customization, readability, and feasibility. These tasks will assist us in gathering valuable data to determine the effectiveness of our web accessibility enhancements and pinpoint areas where we can improve further.

Task 1: Navigating the Improved Website

   - Description: Ask users to navigate your website with the implemented accessibility improvements. Provide a specific scenario, such as finding information on a product or service.

   - Objective: Evaluate how well users can navigate and access information compared to the original website design.

Task 2: Customizing Color & Themes

   - Description: Allow users to customize color themes on your website to suit their preferences and needs, mainly focusing on users with color deficiencies.

   - Objective: Assess how effectively users can adapt the website's visual elements to their specific requirements.

Task 3: Assessing Content Readability

   - Description: Provide users with sample web content and ask them to rate the readability and comprehension of the text. This can include articles, product descriptions, or instructional materials.

   - Objective: Determine if the content structure and design changes have improved readability for users with learning disabilities.

Task 4: Interaction as Different Personas

   - Description: Assign users different personas (e.g., a visually impaired student, a colorblind artist) and ask them to interact with your website. Have them complete a specific task that aligns with their assigned persona.

   - Objective: Evaluate how well the website caters to users with diverse accessibility needs and personas.

Task 5: User Feedback on High-Fidelity Prototypes

   - Description: Present users with high-fidelity prototypes of web pages containing your accessibility improvements. Ask them to provide feedback on usability, clarity, and overall satisfaction.

   - Objective: Gather user feedback to fine-tune your designs and ensure they meet user expectations.

Last but not least, we must keep detailed records of time and resources used for web accessibility improvements and continuously monitor team progress, and also keep in mind our priorities and change them as long as needed to ensure our final deliverable adheres to schedule and resource allocation and gets accepted by our client (i.e. Webmasters in Learning Team at McGill University).

Observer briefing

Role and responsibilities

As an observer, your responsibility is to make observations on the users when they perform different tasks on the user interface during the usability test. You should help identify problems and opportunities to improve the design interface so that it meets the following usability goals: 

During observation

Please note that you must not be seen or heard by the users. Based on your observations on the users, you should take notes on the provided data collection sheet. Please read the data collection sheet in order to familiarize yourself with the data requirements. Specific details of how users interact with the interface and their comments should be clearly recorded. Any form of analysis should only be done after the observation.

After observation

All members of the observation team should get together and discuss their results from the observation. Analysis should be done in order to identify trends and gain useful insights to  improve the design interface.

Training documentation

General

You will be directed to perform tasks on a UI that we have designed. The UI being tested is an education website. You are encouraged to act as you would normally do: take breaks when you want, take your time if you need it, explore the interface as you like.

We are going to use the result to understand how prospective users would act and see how well the UI of the website works. The whole process will be filmed. If you have any questions or issues with the recording, you may voice your concerns and we will do our best to accommodate them.

The facilitator will present you the tasks in order, both orally and in written form. Should you need help or clarification at any point, you are welcome to talk to the facilitator or any other person present. You are however encouraged to solely focus on the system. You will be provided with some pen and paper should you want to scribble anything down. You are also welcome to voice any thoughts about the UI or the testing process.

PLEASE NOTE: We are testing how well the design works not how well you do. You do not need to rush to complete the tasks. Please act naturally and take the actions you would normally do.

Paper prototypes

You will be presented with pieces of paper that will represent the UI of the website. You should consider them to be functioning pieces of the website that is interactable. You are encouraged to think of the paper as the screen. The people present will change the interface as you perform tasks on the paper prototype. There might be some delay between your input and the changes.

You will be using your fore-finger (or another finger if you are more comfortable) as your cursor and tapping it for click. You will use two fingers for right click. For typing, you will use the provided pencil to write.

You should be performing your inputs instead of telling the actions you intend to do. Eg. You should tap an icon instead of saying that you are going to click it.

Test script

General guidelines

Introduce yourself and the test. Allow the user to be at ease and provide any possible accommodations to the requests that they may have. Make sure they have some knowledge of the user documentation and go through it briefly if needed.

Do not interrupt users or tell them what path to take. When asked for help, try to clarify what went wrong and nudge users in the right direction by telling them what they can try. If the user is frustrated, you can guide them more thoroughly. If the user is excessively frustrated, you should stop the task that is causing them stress and ask them questions about what went wrong.

Questions if user asks for help

  1. At what point in the process, did you get stuck?
  2. What did you expect to happen?
  3. Did you try XYZ?

Questions if the task needs to stop prematurely

  1. Do you need a break? Do you need something to calm down?
  2. What point caused you to be stuck/confused?
  3. Is there a specific thing in the design that made you need to stop?
  4. If you had to do that task again, how do you think it would go?

General questions

  1. Can you explain to me the steps you took?
  2. What did you think about when you had to do [the task]?
  3. Can you tell me why you chose to do XYZ instead of ABC?
  4. What do you expect would happen once you finish [the task]?
  5. What would help you to finish [the task] faster?
  6. Was there another method you found/thought of that would complete [the task]?
  7. What thoughts do you have on the design (specific or in general)?

REB requirements

  1. Study Description: a summary of what your project involves with regard to anticipated testing
    The content of the study will be for the users to respond to a web interface and perform tasks using it as directed by the observer and the associated tests. Users will have to perform the tasks on either paper prototypes coordinated by the observer or on a web front end. In the latter case, the user will be given access to the required set up for a PC (mouse, keyboard, screen, etc).
    The purpose of the study is to gauge the effectiveness of the designs in terms of accessibility and education.
  2. Participant number and type (male/female, age range, other particulars such as athletes, farmers, musicians, etc.)
    We expect to have participants of both genders. Our target demographic are students. So, most of the expected age would be 18-35, considering undergraduate, masters and PhD students. We are also considering extending that age to an older age as we want to consider people who want to continue their studies later in life.
  3. How and from where are they recruited?
    We expect to get participants from our existing connections: peers, family and friends. We are considering using online forums to look for willing participants. Another prospective option is to contact the McGill Office for Students with Disabilities and see if any of the students currently affiliated with OSD are willing to participate in the studies.
  4. What risk could this study entail for participants?
    With the demographic we are trying to reach (especially people with learning impediments), the test could sometimes be stressful. Sometimes, this will be  
    We do not foresee any additional issues for the paper prototype testing.
    For testing the UI on a screen, some individuals with visual impairment might experience discomfort in their eyes for various reasons, such as overexposure to a bright screen.
  5. How can these risks be mitigated?
    To mitigate stress on participants, we expect the observer and facilitator to be welcoming and willing to change the environment to some extent to satisfy the user's request. We also want to offer the option of the user doing the tests and recording themselves doing it. This will allow them to do it on their own time and as they feel less stressed.
    For users with discomfort in their eyes during the test on a screen, we would like to offer a break from the study should they need it. We also want to limit the time of the test. We also want to have eye drops on hand should it be of any use to the participants.
